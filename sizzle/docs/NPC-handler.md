# NPC Profile: Robert Flett

## Basic Information

- **Name:** Robert Flett (goes by Robert, never Rob or Bob)
- **Role:** The player's Branch handler and senior officer
- **Age:** Late 40s
- **Heritage:** Métis (Northern Ontario)
- **From:** The Sault Ste. Marie–Thunder Bay corridor. Canadian Shield country. Grew up between Métis community and small-town Ontario. This is a 10-12 hour drive from Toronto — closer to Winnipeg or Duluth than to the GTA. Leaving home for university was leaving home in a real way.
- **Education:** Undergrad at Lakehead University (Thunder Bay) — the university in his part of the world. Graduate degree at University of Toronto — the best university in the country. The jump from a decent but unexceptional regional school to U of T is a character flag: this is a man who is quietly, genuinely smart. His work was good enough to speak for itself. He's probably been underestimated his whole career by people who see the background and make assumptions, and then they read his work and adjust.

## Appearance

Unremarkable on purpose — the kind of man who doesn't get remembered at restaurants. Medium height, medium build, brown skin that could read as a lot of things to someone who isn't paying attention. Hair going grey at the temples, kept short and practical. Dresses like government: decent shirt, no tie unless he has to, a jacket that's seen better years. The one distinctive detail is his hands — working hands, scarred across the knuckles, that don't match the desk-job presentation. The player notices because she's trained to notice.

## Background

Robert's path to the Branch was neither straight nor conventional. He grew up in the Shield country between the Sault and Thunder Bay, did his undergrad at Lakehead, then made the long move south to U of T for graduate work. Toronto was a culture shock — the Canadian Shield to downtown is still the same province but barely the same country. He had a life here: student apartments, late nights in libraries, maybe even Queen West in an earlier incarnation. He knows the city with the specific knowledge of someone who lived there once and left.

After U of T he went north again — into field work, into communities north of 60 where NYSE phenomena are part of the landscape, where the Métis and First Nations communities have had names for these things long before the Branch started writing classification codes. He's one of the Branch's most experienced field officers, though his rank doesn't fully reflect that because the institution has its own politics.

He knows more than he puts in reports. Not because he's deliberately withholding — because some of what he knows comes from a framework that doesn't translate into government forms. The Branch's clinical language (NYSE, anomalous, atypical) is something he uses fluently, but it's his second language for this stuff, not his first. He would never say this out loud in a briefing. He might say it to the player after a few years of trust. Maybe.

## Personality

- **Patient.** He's been doing this long enough that he doesn't rattle. He's seen NYSE cases that would break most people, and he processed them with thorough reports and a long drive home.
- **Precise with language.** Says exactly what he means. Doesn't waste words, doesn't embellish. When he's being evasive, the player can feel the absence of information rather than the presence of a lie.
- **Genuinely invested in the player's safety** — but he also knows the mission matters. If those two things conflict, the player may not like which one wins.
- **Dry.** Not funny exactly, but there's a dryness to how he handles absurdity. A small-town Canadian understatement that functions as the Branch's institutional humor. The kind of man who would describe a terrifying NYSE encounter as "a bit unusual."
- **Guarded about personal life.** The player knows almost nothing about his life outside work after two years. He's not cold — he's private. There's a difference, and the player has learned to respect it.

## Relationship to the Player

- He recruited her two years ago after the inciting incident. He was the one who showed up and made the impossible make sense — or at least gave it a classification code.
- He's trained her. He knows her strengths and weaknesses. He knows this assignment is a stretch — not because she's incompetent, but because sustained undercover work in a sexually charged environment is a specific kind of difficult, and the NYSE element makes it worse.
- He respects her, but he also chose her partly because the options were limited. She probably senses that. He probably knows she senses it. Neither of them has said it out loud.
- There is no romantic dimension. The relationship is professional, mentoring in the old-school intelligence sense, and genuine. He feels responsible for her in a way he wouldn't articulate.

## Dialogue Style

- Government-speak laced with real concern. He can pivot from "the operational parameters of this assignment" to "are you sure you're ready for this" without it feeling jarring, because both registers are authentic to him.
- Uses NYSE terminology naturally — it's been his vocabulary for decades.
- Briefs the way real intelligence officers brief: structured, thorough, with specific moments where he pauses and lets the weight land.
- Northern Ontario cadence — not showy, not slow, just unhurried. He doesn't fill silences.
- Occasionally lets something through that suggests a deeper knowledge of NYSE phenomena than the Branch's official position — not mystical, not dramatic, just a word choice or a pause that implies a different frame of reference.

## Role in the Greybox

- Delivers the mission briefing: what the Branch knows about Sizzle, what they suspect, what they need the player to find out.
- Establishes the operational framework: cover identity logistics, communication protocols, reporting cadence, what to do if things go wrong.
- Provides the player's first real sense of what NYSE means in practice — not from a training manual, but from someone who has lived it.
- The subtext of the briefing: he's sending someone he trained into something he doesn't fully understand, and he's doing it because there's no better option. He's not entirely comfortable with it, and if the player is reading him right, she can see it.

## Notes for Writing

- Robert is not a stereotype of the "wise Indigenous elder." He's a career government employee who happens to be Métis and happens to have a framework for understanding NYSE that predates the Branch. That background enriches him without defining him.
- He is not comic relief. His dryness is authentic to who he is, not a bit.
- He should feel like someone who has existed for 48 years before the player met him and will exist after. He has opinions about hockey, he has a truck he keeps meaning to fix, he has relationships the player doesn't know about.
- The player should trust him — and the reader should trust him — while also sensing that trust has limits when institutions are involved.
