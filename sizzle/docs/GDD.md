## 1. Premise & Pitch

**Logline:** A government employee is recruited by a secret federal agency to go undercover at an exclusive swingers club in 2005 Toronto, where something beyond rational explanation is happening to the people who go inside.

**Elevator pitch:** Sizzle is an adult interactive fiction game about infiltration, identity, and desire. The player takes on a cover identity and gets a job at Sizzle, a members-only swingers club on Queen Street West. Their mission: find out what's really going on beneath the surface. What begins as a straightforward intelligence operation becomes something stranger — the club's influence over its members goes deeper than social dynamics, and the line between genuine attraction and supernatural compulsion starts to blur. The player must navigate relationships, maintain their cover, pursue the investigation, and decide how much of themselves they're willing to lose to get answers.

**The hook:** Every relationship is compromised. The player is never fully themselves with anyone — not with the people at Sizzle (who know the cover identity), not with their handler (who may not be telling the full truth), and eventually not with themselves (as the club's influence takes hold). The erotic content isn't decoration — it's the terrain the game is played on.

## 2. Setting

### Toronto, 2005/2006

The game is set in a very specific version of Toronto. This is the city before smartphones, before the condo boom fully reshaped the skyline and the neighborhoods. Key characteristics of the era:

- **Internet exists, but it's at home.** People carry flip phones, maybe a BlackBerry if they're corporate. The internet is real — you just need to go home and open your laptop. You can't google someone at dinner, but you can research them tonight. Information still moves at the speed of conversation and observation when you're out in the world.
- **No social media panopticon.** Facebook exists but is still a college thing. MySpace is for bands. People can still disappear into a scene without a digital trail. You can live a double life much more plausibly than you could two years later.
- **Queen West is a contradiction.** The Drake Hotel opened in 2004 and is accelerating the gentrification, but the neighborhood hasn't flipped yet — and it's fighting back. Graffiti tags calling out the Drake by name. Walk two blocks from the trendy up-and-coming strip and you're in what feels like a demilitarized zone; walk two more and you're in bougie condoland. A lingerie store has topless dancers in the window during the day in full public view. Queen Video, a hipster video store, files the porn in with the regular movies — alphabetically, because that's just how things are here. Art galleries next to dive bars next to vintage shops next to places you wouldn't walk past alone at night. West Queen West is the nexus of all of it.
- **Toronto is diverse and stratified.** The city's multiculturalism is genuine but the power structures are still largely old-money and old-network. A swingers club is one of the few places where the social strata mix freely — under the shared protection of mutual secrecy.
- **The TTC is how you get around.** Streetcars on Queen, subway at Osgoode or St. Andrew. Taxis if you're in a hurry. No Uber, no rideshare.

### Key Locations

| Location | Prefix | Description |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| Sizzle | `SZ` | The club itself. Queen West. Victorian-era corner building, red accents, warm lighting. Multiple floors and areas with increasing levels of access/intimacy. |
| Queen Street West | `QW` | The surrounding neighborhood. Shops, bars, restaurants, the streetcar. The player's public life happens here. |

## 3. The Branch

### What it is

A secret division within the Canadian federal government responsible for tracking, studying, and containing phenomena classified as "not yet scientifically explained" (NYSE, pronounced "nigh-see" by everyone at the Branch). The Branch has no public-facing name. Internally it's referred to simply as "the Branch" — a bureaucratic habit that stuck.

### Culture

The Branch is more bureaucratic than anyone would expect from a secret agency that deals with the inexplicable. This is deliberate — the institutional mundanity is a coping mechanism and a containment strategy. If you treat the impossible as paperwork, it becomes manageable.

- **Forms.** Everything is documented. Expense reports, field observations, incident classifications. There are forms for things that shouldn't need forms. There is also the sneaking suspicion — never confirmed, never denied — that half of those documents are almost immediately destroyed by someone further up the chain. The bureaucracy is real, but it may also be a performance.
- **Terminology.** Nothing is ever "supernatural," "paranormal," or "magical." It is NYSE, "anomalous," "atypical." The language is clinical, cautious, deliberately drained of wonder.
- **Hierarchy.** Standard government org chart. The player has a handler. The handler reports up. Somewhere above that, decisions are made that the player isn't told about.
- **Tone.** The Branch should feel like a real government agency that real people work at — the mundane procedural texture of Service Canada or the CRA crossed with the professional secrecy culture of CSIS. Not a caricature, not played for laughs, not reducible to a pithy shorthand. The people who work here have mortgages, commutes, office birthdays, security clearances, and institutional pride. They take their work seriously because the work is serious. They also have case files that describe things that shouldn't exist, and they handle those with the same professionalism they bring to everything else. The Branch needs verisimilitude and lived-in texture — the reader should feel like this agency has existed for decades, with its own internal politics, its own institutional memory, its own culture of quiet competence. If dark humor exists, it's the kind that real professionals develop over years of doing difficult work together, not comedy for the audience's benefit.

### Scale

The Canadian Branch is quietly the largest NYSE operation in the world after Russia's. This makes sense — vast unpopulated northern territory, long winters, deep wilderness, old land, Indigenous communities that have always known about these things. Most Branch activity happens far from major cities: remote communities, northern outposts, the territories.

The fact that the Sizzle situation is happening in Toronto — as far south as the Branch typically operates — is noted internally as unusual. Not unprecedented, but unusual. Something operating this openly in a major city is either very confident or very desperate.

### The player's relationship to the Branch

The player was a government employee in another capacity (chosen during character creation) before being recruited. They were identified because of an inciting incident (also chosen during character creation) that brought them to the Branch's attention. They are new to this world. The Branch is their employer, their lifeline, and possibly not telling them everything.

## 4. Sizzle

### The club

Sizzle is a members-only swingers club occupying a Victorian-era corner building on West Queen West. The building has heritage brick, arched windows, a turret at the corner, warm uplighting at night, and red accents on the ground floor. From the outside, it's handsome but discreet — the name "Sizzle" in cursive neon above the entrance is the only public signifier.

Inspired by the real club WICKED that operated in West Queen West during this era.

### Vibe

Sophisticated, not sleazy — but this is a place for enthusiasts. The members of Sizzle are not the trenchcoat perverts of stereotype. They might better be described as "sex nerds" — people obsessed with connoisseur details and knowledge, who have opinions about technique and etiquette, who have read the books and attended the workshops and can debate the finer points. The community has its own vocabulary, its own reference points, its own hierarchies of expertise. Blending in here isn't just about being comfortable with sexuality — it's about passing as someone who belongs in a subculture with real depth.

The aesthetic is warm, dimly lit, tasteful. Good drinks, good music. The staff are professional and the rules are enforced. There is a genuine culture of consent and discretion — which makes whatever is happening underneath all the more unsettling, because the surface-level club works exactly as advertised.

### Access levels

The club has layers, both physical and social:

| Level | Access | Description |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| 0 | Public-facing | The bar/lounge area. Members and guests can socialize. Clothed, social, relatively normal. |
| 1 | Members only | The main club floor. Where the sexual element becomes overt. Requires membership and vetting. |
| 2 | Inner circle | Private rooms, invitation-only events. Requires trust and social standing within the club. |
| 3 | The underneath | Whatever is really going on. The player doesn't know this exists at the start. |

The player begins as staff (access level 0-1) and must build reputation and trust to penetrate deeper.

## 5. The Supernatural

### Classification

The Branch classifies the activity at Sizzle as NYSE — not yet scientifically explained. Internally, the working hypothesis involves anomalous influence on cognition and desire.

### Manifestation

The supernatural element is not magic spells, monsters, or overt fantasy. It is subtle, insidious, and intimately tied to the club's erotic atmosphere. It manifests as:

- **Influence.** People at Sizzle become more suggestible, more open, more willing to cross boundaries they normally wouldn't — and it escalates. This isn't just a one-time loosening of inhibitions. It's progressive. A person who was relatively straight-laced six months ago is now doing things they wouldn't have been able to imagine, with many intermediate steps along the way. Each step felt like a natural extension of the last. The escalation is the signature — it's too consistent, too directional to be social dynamics alone.
- **Compulsion.** Branch intelligence — gathered through developed assets and secondhand sources, not direct reports from members — indicates that regular members experience intrusive thoughts, desires that feel implanted rather than organic, attraction to specific people that doesn't match their usual patterns. This information comes from assets cultivated around the club's orbit: a therapist treating a member, a spouse who noticed changes, an acquaintance who was recruited as an informant. The members themselves are not aware they're being observed.
- **Altered perception.** Time feels different inside the club. Memories of specific events are vivid in some ways and strangely vague in others. Some members have gaps.
- **Dreams.** People who spend time at Sizzle report unusually vivid dreams, often erotic, often involving other members or the club itself.

### The player's experience

The player is not immune. As they spend more time at Sizzle, they experience these effects themselves. This intersects directly with gameplay:

- Is the attraction to an NPC genuine, or is it the influence?
- Is a choice the player is making a free choice, or a compelled one?
- Can the player trust their own perceptions inside the club?

### The player's resistance

The reason the Branch chose the player for this assignment is practical, not mystical. They need a believably attractive woman under 30 who is decently trained and can credibly get a job at — and blend into — an upscale swingers club. The Branch's talent pool that fits those criteria is small. The player character has been at the Branch for two years at game start: she's completed training, she understands NYSE classification, she's been briefed on cases. She is not a rookie. But this is her first major field assignment, and nothing in training fully prepares you for sustained undercover work.

The inciting incident (chosen during character creation) explains why the player is at the Branch at all — it's the event that brought her to their attention and led to her recruitment two years prior. It may suggest an additional resistance or affinity (or both) to NYSE phenomena, but that is secondary to the mundane personnel reason she got this specific assignment.

## 6. Player Character

### What's fixed

- **Gender:** Female (required by the assignment — the Branch needs a credibly attractive woman under 30 for this operation)
- **Nationality:** Canadian
- **Employer:** The Branch (recruited from another government position)
- **Assignment:** Infiltrate Sizzle

### What the player chooses

| Choice | Screen | Effect |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Name | CC-100 | First name and surname. Used for real identity. |
| Appearance | CC-200 | Skin tone (3), hair colour (3), hair style (3), eye colour (3). Drives avatar sprite selection. |
| Pre-Branch background | CC-300 | What the player was doing before the Branch recruited them. Four options: RCMP constable, CSIS analyst, grad student (psychology), unemployed after university. Influences starting skill levels. |
| Inciting incident | CC-400 | Why the Branch recruited them. May grant a trait, establish backstory, and determine relationship to NYSE phenomena. |
| Cover identity | *In-game* | The persona used inside Sizzle. Not defined during character creation — developed a few passages into the main story, possibly partially assigned by the Branch and partially crafted by the player. |

### Skills

| Skill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Academic | Research, analysis, institutional knowledge. Reading documents, understanding systems. |
| Agent | Tradecraft, surveillance, operational procedure. The spy skill. |
| Athlete | Physical fitness, endurance, reflexes. Running, climbing, reacting. |
| Confrontation | Physical and psychological intimidation. Fighting, but also standing your ground, calling someone's bluff, staring someone down, projecting threat. The spectrum from a hard stare to a thrown punch. |
| Charmer | Social manipulation, persuasion, seduction, reading people. |
| Streetwise | Street smarts, criminal knowledge, reading situations, knowing the city. |
| Composure | Emotional control, resisting influence, maintaining cover under pressure. Particularly important against NYSE effects. |
| Sexology | Knowledge of sexual culture, practices, etiquette, and terminology. The skill that determines whether you can pass as someone who belongs at Sizzle. At low levels: basic vocabulary (dom/sub, safe words). At higher levels: etiquette, technique, subculture knowledge (what poppers do, how a play party is structured, the difference between a lifestyle club and a hookup bar). This skill has natural progression tiers that mirror the club's access levels. |

Skills start at -4 and are modified by the player's pre-Branch background choice. Composure is the key defensive skill against the club's supernatural influence. Sexology is the key social skill for operating inside Sizzle.

### Key state variables

- `$player.cover` — the cover identity used inside Sizzle (defined in-story, not during creation)
- `$player.arousal` — current arousal level (0-3), influenced by encounters and NYSE effects
- `$player.statusEffects` — **array** of active temporary modifiers, supports multiple simultaneous effects (e.g., ["Caffeinated", "Flustered", "Influenced"]). Effects are added/removed as situations change.
- `$player.kinks` — **array** of character preferences, accumulated during play based on choices. Multiple kinks can be active simultaneously and influence available options and NPC reactions.
- `$player.quirks` — **array** of personality traits, set during creation or early game. Multiple quirks can coexist and shape dialogue and internal narration.
- `$sizzle.suspicion` — how suspicious the club/its operators are of the player
- `$sizzle.accessLevel` — current depth of access (0-3)
- `$sizzle.reputation` — social standing within the club

## 7. Core Gameplay Loop

The game operates on a day/night cycle. The player's time is divided between:

### Daytime

- **Cover life.** Maintaining the appearance of a normal person in Toronto. Apartment, neighborhood, errands. This is where the player breathes between missions.
- **Handler meetings.** Debriefs with the Branch. Receiving new objectives, reporting findings, getting intel. The institutional side of the operation.
- **Investigation.** Following leads outside the club. Visiting locations, researching people, tracking down information. The mystery/investigation gameplay.

### Nighttime

- **Sizzle.** Working at the club (the player's job there), socializing with members, building relationships, pursuing access to deeper levels. This is where the infiltration, social, and erotic gameplay converge.

### The loop

```
Day: Wake → handler/investigation/cover life → prepare for evening
     ↓
Night: Go to Sizzle → work/socialize/investigate → encounters → return home
     ↓
Late night: Process what happened → dreams? → journal/debrief
     ↓
Next day: New information, new objectives, consequences of last night's choices
```

### Pacing & freedom

Early in the game, the loop is more kinetic and guided than it will be later. The player is new in town, new at the job, following Branch instructions. Each time slot tends to have a specific event or objective — there's less freedom to choose how to spend your time because the story is still establishing the world, the characters, and the player's footing. This serves both narrative and design purposes: the player learns the systems and the setting through structured early beats rather than being dropped into an open sandbox.

As the game progresses and the player establishes themselves — builds relationships, gains access, develops leads — the loop opens up. More choices about how to spend daytime hours. More agency about what to pursue at Sizzle on a given night. The shift from guided to open mirrors the character's arc from new recruit following orders to embedded operative making judgment calls.

### Passage of time

Time advances through player choices, not in real-time. Each major action (going to a location, having a conversation, attending an event) advances the clock. The `$date` variable tracks the current date and time. Certain events are date-gated — things happen on specific nights, seasons change, the investigation has a tempo.

## 8. Key Mechanics

### Cover & Suspicion

The player operates under a cover identity inside Sizzle. Maintaining it is an active gameplay challenge:

- **Consistency.** NPCs remember what you've told them. Contradictions raise suspicion.
- **Behavior.** Acting out of character for your cover raises flags. A supposed bartender who asks too many questions about the private rooms will get noticed.
- **Clothing.** What you wear affects how people perceive you. Dressing wrong for the context is suspicious.
- **Composure checks.** Skill checks when the player encounters something that threatens their cover — a surprising revelation, a recognized face, a direct challenge.

`$sizzle.suspicion` tracks cumulative suspicion. High suspicion limits access, triggers confrontations, and can end the operation.

### Relationships

NPCs have individual relationship tracks. Relationships are built through conversation, shared experiences, and — in the context of Sizzle — physical intimacy. Each relationship exists in tension:

- The NPC knows the cover identity. The player knows their real self. Every interaction is layered.
- Some relationships are instrumental (advancing the investigation). Some become genuine. The player may not always know which is which.
- Relationships unlock information, access, and story branches.

*[NPC roster and relationship system to be developed in Task #8.]*

### Skill Checks

Skill checks use the `<<rollDice>>` macro. The pattern:

1. A situation requires a check (e.g., composure check when confronted, charmer check when flirting for information).
2. Roll NdS + skill modifier vs. difficulty threshold.
3. Pass/fail determines the outcome, with narrative consequences.

Checks are not purely random — skill level meaningfully shifts the odds. A skilled charmer will reliably succeed at easy social challenges but still sweat the hard ones.

### Arousal

`$player.arousal` (0-3) tracks the player character's current state:

| Level | State | Effect |
|-------|-------|--------|
| 0 | Baseline | Clear-headed. No modifiers. |
| 1 | Aware | Slightly distracted. Minor composure penalty in social situations. |
| 2 | Aroused | Meaningfully affected. Charmer bonuses but composure and academic penalties. Certain dialogue options open up. |
| 3 | Overwhelmed | Struggling to focus. Significant penalties to non-physical checks. Some choices may be locked or forced. Vulnerability to NYSE influence increases. |

Arousal is influenced by encounters, NPC interactions, clothing state, and NYSE effects. It can be managed through player choices (leaving a situation, composure checks, cold water — literally).

### NYSE Influence

Two hidden variables drive the supernatural mechanics. Neither is visible to the player in the UI — their effects are felt through changes in available choices, narration, and check modifiers.

**`$nyse.influence`** — tracks how much the player has been affected by the club's NYSE phenomena.

- **Not displayed** to the player. The player feels the effects without seeing a number.
- **Goes up and down**, but does not decay naturally. If left alone, it stays where it is. Reducing influence requires active player choices (specific actions, not just time away from the club).
- **Starting value** is determined by the inciting incident chosen during character creation. Some backgrounds start influence higher, some lower. This is the only effect of the player's "resistance" — it sets the starting position, not the rate of gain or loss.
- **High influence effects:**
  - Altered choices — new options appear (temptations, impulses), some cautious options may be locked or feel less natural.
  - Altered perception — the narration itself becomes less reliable. Descriptions shift in tone, details the player noticed before may be missed or distorted.
  - Mechanical effects — penalties to composure checks, bonuses to certain social/sexual interactions (the influence makes you more attuned but less controlled).

**`$nyse.power`** — tracks the player character developing mind control abilities of their own.

- **Does not come into play until later in the game.** Early game, this variable is effectively dormant.
- **Not displayed** to the player initially. The player may not understand what's happening to them when it begins to manifest.
- *[TO BE DEVELOPED — how power is gained, how it's used, what it costs, and the moral/narrative consequences of using it. This is a major late-game system that fundamentally changes the player's relationship to the investigation and to the people around them.]*

### Time & Calendar

The `$date` object tracks year, month, day, hour, and day of week. Time matters:

- Sizzle operates on specific nights (not every night).
- Certain events happen on specific dates.
- The investigation has a tempo — leads go cold, opportunities expire.
- Seasons change (September → winter in Toronto is a meaningful atmospheric shift).

## 9. Narrative Structure

### Act structure

**Prologue: Recruitment**
- Character creation (name, appearance, background, inciting incident).
- The player's recruitment to the Branch two years prior is established. She's trained, not green.
- Assignment briefing: this is the mission, this is what we know, this is what we need.

**Act 1: Insertion**
- Briefing on Sizzle (surface-level intel). Arrival in downtown Toronto. Setting up shop in the neighbourhood — apartment, getting to know Queen West, establishing the cover life.
- An operation to infiltrate the club's culture and get a job. This likely involves attending a party or event at or adjacent to Sizzle as a civilian/prospective member, making the right impression.
- The world feels like a grounded spy thriller. No NYSE, no weirdness. Just tradecraft, social navigation, and the texture of 2005 Toronto.
- Act ends when the player gets the job at Sizzle.

**Act 2: Establishment**
- First nights working at Sizzle. Learning the layout, meeting staff and regulars, establishing the cover identity inside the club.
- The mundane status quo of the club is established — how it operates, who the regulars are, what a normal night looks like. The player learns the rhythms.
- Parallel life during the day: the player's "normal" existence in Toronto, handler check-ins, the double-life tension.
- The player begins building relationships inside Sizzle — with coworkers, members, management.
- Act ends with the first obvious NYSE incident — something the player cannot explain away.

**Act 3: Immersion**
- NYSE effects begin to manifest and escalate. Subtle at first, then harder to dismiss.
- The investigation begins in earnest. The player is actively pursuing what's beneath the surface.
- Relationships deepen. The line between cover and self blurs.
- Access to deeper levels of the club. The mystery escalates.
- The erotic content intensifies as the player goes deeper.

**Act 4: Revelation**
- The player discovers what's really happening at Sizzle. The supernatural is confirmed beyond doubt.
- Relationships are tested — who can be trusted? Who is compromised? Is the player compromised?
- The Branch's real agenda may diverge from what the player was told.
- The player must make choices about loyalty, identity, and how far they'll go.
- `$nyse.power` begins to manifest — the player is not just a subject of the influence, but developing abilities of their own.

**Act 5: Resolution**
- *[Multiple endings based on accumulated choices, relationships, influence level, power level, and the player's relationship to the Branch, to Sizzle, and to themselves.]*

### Pacing

The game should feel slow at first — atmospheric, character-driven, focused on the texture of Toronto and the social dynamics of the club. Tension builds gradually. The supernatural creeps in at the edges before asserting itself. The erotic content escalates in parallel with the investigation — as the player goes deeper into the club, both the sexual and supernatural stakes intensify.

## 10. Tone & Content Guidelines

### Writing style

- **Classy thriller fiction.** The prose should read like a literate, observant thriller — think the tradition of le Carré, but not locked to any single author's voice. Smart, character-focused writing that trusts the reader. Flexible enough to handle a tense handler meeting, a vivid sex scene, and a quiet walk down Queen Street with equal confidence.
- **Grounded and sensory.** Concrete details over abstraction. Toronto should feel real — the smell of the streetcar, the sound of Queen West on a Friday night, the specific quality of autumn light on brick.
- **Character-driven.** NPCs are people with lives, motivations, contradictions. No one exists solely to advance the plot.
- **Interiority.** The player character has an inner life. The narration should convey what it *feels like* to be undercover, to be attracted to someone you're deceiving, to experience something you can't explain.
- **Period-accurate.** 2005 details matter. Music, technology, slang, politics, what was on TV. Not overdone — not every line needs a period reference — but the era should be felt.
- **Short passages, no scrolling.** Each passage should be 80-120 visible words maximum. The player should never have to scroll the game window. One beat per passage — a moment, a reaction, a short dialogue exchange. Longer scenes are broken into multiple click-through passages. If a passage contains conditional text (background-specific variants), count the longest single variant, not the total source.

### Erotic content guidelines

This game is erotica. Sex scenes should titillate. There should be heat. The game should be genuinely, unapologetically sexy. Porn is not in conflict with plot — both serve story, and story is king.

- **Heat is required.** Sex scenes are not clinical, not fade-to-black, not euphemistic. They are vivid, physical, and written to arouse. The player is here for this and the writing should deliver.
- **Earned heat is hotter.** Sexual content that emerges from character dynamics, relationship history, and story tension hits harder than content that arrives out of nowhere. The buildup matters. Anticipation is a mechanic.
- **Agency matters.** The player should always have meaningful choices about whether and how to engage with sexual content. When agency is compromised — by NYSE influence — that violation is felt precisely because agency was the norm.
- **Characters stay whole.** An NPC the player sleeps with is the same fully realized person before, during, and after. The sex reveals character — who someone is in bed tells you something about who they are.
- **The body is real.** Physical descriptions are grounded and specific, not cartoonishly exaggerated. The avatar system supports this — clothing changes, expressions, physical state are all mechanically tracked.
- **Consent as theme.** The club's surface culture is consent-forward. The supernatural undermines this. That tension is thematic, not incidental — the game is partly *about* consent, influence, and the boundaries between persuasion and compulsion. The eroticism and the horror share the same nerve.

### What the game is NOT

- Not a dating sim with a spy skin
- Not a power fantasy where the player "wins" by sleeping with everyone
- Not grimdark for the sake of it — the darkness serves the story
- Not ashamed of being erotica — the sex is a feature, not a guilty secret

## 11. Scope

### Version 1 (Greybox / Vertical Slice)

The greybox covers the **Prologue** and the **briefing** (the opening beat of Act 1). This is enough to prove the foundation without requiring the full NPC roster, club gameplay, or day/night cycle.

**What's in v1:**

- Character creation (all 5 screens functional: name, appearance, background, inciting incident, summary)
- The player's recruitment context is established (two years at the Branch, trained, not green)
- Branch briefing scene: the handler, the assignment, what's known about Sizzle, what the player is being asked to do
- At least one skill check (likely composure or academic during the briefing)
- The tone is established — the Branch's institutional texture, the writing style, the player character's interiority
- Ends at a natural break point: the player has her assignment and is about to head downtown

**What's NOT in v1:**

- Arrival in Toronto / Queen West (that's Act 1 proper)
- Sizzle interior or any club gameplay
- NPC relationships (beyond the handler)
- Erotic content
- NYSE effects
- Day/night cycle
- Avatar system beyond character creation (no clothing changes, location backgrounds)
- Multiple endings
- Sound/music

### Success criteria for v1

A new player can create a character, meet their handler, receive a briefing that establishes the world and the mission, and finish feeling like they understand the tone of the game, the nature of the Branch, and what they're about to walk into. The writing should make them want to keep going.
