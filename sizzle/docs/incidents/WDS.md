## 0. Design Spine (read first)

- **Convergent branching.** Choices and checks reroute *texture* — how fast she names the cause, how hard the musk pulls at her, how close she comes to going under — never the outcome. Every path ends: she works out that the *smell* is the vector, improvises a filter, drives the creature off with a crude spray, and the guests surface. A failed Composure check = the musk nearly takes her and she claws back. A beat, never a fail state.
- **Felt stakes, fixed plumbing.** The player should believe she might breathe too much and join the others on the gravel. In story terms she can't. In *who she becomes* terms she can — see §8 skill grant.
- **Pre-Branch voice.** August 2003. No NYSE vocabulary, no Branch frame. She has whatever her background gives her (a cop's scene-control, an analyst's cause-and-effect, a psych grad's read of disinhibition, an outsider's animal dread) — never "vector" or "olfactory influence." Those words arrive only in the Branch report at the end.
- **She is not special.** She wakes *late*, after several guests are already gone under, and her advantage is timing plus the practical leap that the smell is the cause. She inhales the musk and feels it work on her. Not immunity — exposure control under a clock.
- **The PC is sex-positive — in every background, in every incident.** (Cross-cutting; see MANITOULIN §0.) The guests' behaviour is sexual, degraded, and unsafe, and her horror is the **chemical reduction of people below the line where they can protect themselves** — not that sex is happening. She is not disgusted by desire; she is terrified that an airborne thing is overwriting judgment and fear in human bodies, including hers. Never frame her dread as prudishness.
- **The affected guests are victims, not a joke.** This is the load-bearing tonal rule. The woman at the vending machine, the people undressing on the gravel, the ones drawn toward the creature — they are ordinary adults reduced by something they inhaled, rendered with humanity that returns when it breaks (someone weeping, someone sick, someone covering themselves). The degradation is the horror; it is never comic and never spectacle.
- **The creature is dread and contamination.** Huge, far too much body hair, always nude, moving on all fours or in a low animal lope, with grotesquely oversized, explicit sexual anatomy — a massive, engorged, semi-hard penis that drools pre-ejaculate as it moves, the source-organ of the musk made visible. The job of the image is to land as **frightening, animal, excessive, and contaminating.** His cleverness is an animal's; his motive is inscrutable, like a bear in a campsite. The Branch documents a large unidentified subject; it never certifies "werewolf."
- **Sell the dread, don't police the heat.** *(Cross-cutting — true for the whole game.)* Our job is to make the horror land: the contamination, the override of will, the degradation. We do **not** write to suppress or correct for arousal. If explicit material also reads as charged to a given player, that is their interpretation and it is fine — we aren't doing it for that, but we don't need to steer away from it either. Aim at the dread; don't aim away from the heat.
- **The PC can be funny; the situation never is.** *(Cross-cutting.)* The incident, the creature, and the victims are never played for laughs — the degradation is real and horrifying. But the **player character's voice** can be dry, sardonic, gallows-wry: a woman who copes with terror through clipped, mordant interior observation. Her wit is character and relief valve, never a joke at a victim's expense.

### NYSE grammar (how this differs from the first three)

The Blackout was an *environmental* anomaly (**add** light). Manitoulin was a *focal-marking* anomaly (**break** the pattern). The Pale Eyes was a *rule hazard* (**outpace/commit** past the rule's latency). Wet Dog Smell is a **vector / contamination hazard** — an invisible airborne agent with a physical, mobile source. You can't add to it, break it, or outpace it. You **reduce your dose and drive the source away**: a wet cloth that only *partly* filters an oil-based musk, and a crude spray to repel both the creature and the people fixed on it. The verb here is **filter / drive off** — exposure-control logic, hazmat thinking under animal panic. Keep all four grammatically distinct so the incidents don't read as "weird shit happened" four times.

## 1. Playable Sequence Premise

August 2003, the dead middle of the night, a roadside motel two or three hundred kilometres from anywhere she calls home. Highway hum, the buzz of a vending machine, gravel, thin curtains, the smell of hot asphalt cooling. The player character is here alone, away from home (background-dependent), and wakes to a sound that's wrong. Through the curtain: doors standing open, guests out on the gravel court — undressing, swaying, drawn toward each other and toward something at the dark edge of the lot. By the ice machine, a woman stripped from the waist down is grinding herself against a soda can she can't force inside herself — soaked, whimpering, desperate, and not home behind the eyes. The first wrong thing isn't the sex; it's that none of these people are *home* behind their eyes. Then the player character catches the smell — wet dog, rut, something oily and animal — and feels it answer in her own body, a throb and a fog rolling over her judgment. At the edge of the light she sees the source: a huge, over-haired, nude figure that moves like an animal and that she *knows*, looking at it, is not naturally a man. She makes the leap no one else made: **the smell is doing this.** She wets a cloth and breathes through it (it helps, not enough — the musk is oily and gets through), and with a can of spray she drives the fixed guests back off the creature and the creature back off the people, until it breaks and runs into the dark beyond the highway light. The guests come back to themselves, sick and ashamed and missing time. She's the clearest one there, still half-dosed and shaking, and she can still smell it on her own clothes when the Branch's questions arrive.

**One-line for the player:** *You woke to a smell and understood, before anyone else could, that the smell was the thing — and that covering your face and driving it off was the only medicine you had.*

## 3. Opening Setup & Background Variants

**Universal frame (WDS-085 → WDS-100):** the same kind of cheap roadside motel, the same arrival-and-settle table-setting, the same dead-of-night wake-up into a court full of reduced guests. All four backgrounds are **staying at the motel, alone, away from home** (vacation/time alone). The variant changes *which road/region she's on* and *what her attention is trained to do* — not the events. (Per the established rule: a shared anchor situation, divergent reasons for being there.)

- **RCMP constable — `Northern Ontario`:** A roadside motel out past the Sault / Northern Ontario home country — highways she knows. She reads the lot as a scene to control: who's in danger, where's the threat, what's the cause. Her eye is for hazard and she's the one most plausibly already carrying **dog/coyote spray** for the road. Edge: scene-control under chaos.
- **CSIS analyst — `City Dweller`:** A motel outside Ottawa / the NCR on a trip. Desk-trained, not field-trained — she *infers*: dose tracks proximity, the smell is the variable, isolate the source. Edge: she names the vector fastest.
- **Grad student (psychology) — `City Dweller`, `Lived in Toronto`:** A motel up the highway **north of Toronto** — cottage-country-adjacent — on a trip. She reads it as *behaviour* — disinhibition, lost fear response, chemical override — and has real pre-Branch academic vocabulary for it, which makes the moment it exceeds those rules worse, not better.
- **Unemployed after university — `City Dweller`, `Lived in Toronto`:** A motel on the highway **north of Toronto** on a trip. The outsider with no professional frame — most viscerally exposed, fewest defenses; her edge is stubborn nerve and noticing, not training.

**Voice discipline:** same woman, different prior years. One or two sentences of shading per branch, parallel structure. All four share the wake-up-to-a-wrong-court spine and the inhaled-dose clock; they differ only in what her attention is trained to catch and (RCMP) how plausibly armed she starts.

## 4. Escalation Pattern

Two pressures climb together against a clock:

**Comprehension (the invisible cause):** wrong sound → reduced guests → the smell on the air → *the smell is the vector* → dose tracks proximity to the source → exposure control is the move. Each step is more dangerous to act on, because acting means breathing more.

**Her own dose (how far under she's slipping):** first whiff (a throb, a fog) → clothes wrong on her skin, fear going quiet → a real pull toward the others → fighting it behind a half-working filter while she works. Unlike the Pale Eyes' single spike, this is a **continuous, rising dose** she can only *slow*, never stop — the filter is partial because the musk is oily. That continuous exposure is the engine of the tension and the reason her window is short.

**The clock:** every passage she spends understanding is a passage she spends breathing it. The intervention has to come before her own judgment goes the way the guests' did.

**Eroticism + horror:** the charge is in the contamination and the degradation, never endorsed by the narrator (STYLE-GUIDE §13.5). The guests are people being reduced below safety; the creature is dread, not desire; her own arousal is the alarm, not an invitation. The reader should feel the wrongness underneath any heat.

## 5. Player Choices & Skill Checks

All convergent. Difficulties low-to-moderate; failure is always survivable and generative.

### Choices
- **WDS-140 — Run or stay.** *Run* → she gets to the car and can't turn the key on a lot full of people who'll be dead or worse by morning; she turns back. *Stay* → straight to it. Same destination; different self-image (talked herself into it vs. never in doubt).
- **WDS-145 — What she soaks for a filter (cosmetic).** Bathroom towel under the tap / her own shirt / ice-bucket water. All produce "a wet cloth that helps, not enough." Pure flavor.
- **WDS-150 — Secondary tool (cosmetic).** Dog spray (the anti-coyote can) is the core on every path; the flavor pick is what she pairs it with — car horn + headlights to disorient, a fire extinguisher's blast, a tire iron she never has to swing.

### Skill checks (use `<<skillCheck>>`, onward links in both branches)
- **WDS-120 — Keep her head on the first dose (Composure, low DC).** *Pass:* she stays clear, keeps reading. *Fail:* the fog rolls harder; she's slower, more exposed. Both continue.
- **WDS-130 — Name the vector (Academic or Streetwise, low DC).** *Pass:* she *reasons* it — dose tracks the smell, isolate the source. *Fail:* she gets there by luck/instinct a beat later (she notices she thinks more clearly with her face turned away). Both → WDS-135. The "ingenuity vs. luck" fork; both recruitment-worthy; differs only in prose texture and which skill earns the +1 (§8).
- **WDS-135 — Don't go under (Composure, moderate DC).** *Pass:* she holds and keeps moving. *Fail:* she drifts a step toward the others, starts to pull at her own collar, catches herself; +1 `$nyse.influence`. Both → WDS-140.
- **WDS-160 — Drive it off at close range (Composure or Confrontation, higher DC). The spine check.** *Pass:* she holds through the musk and the fear and gets the spray off clean. *Fail:* she nearly goes under at arm's length — fear gone, judgment swimming — but gets the can up and fires anyway and it recoils. **Never a loss: the creature breaks and runs on every path; the check only sets how close she came and what the dose leaves behind.** +1 `$nyse.influence` on fail.

## 6. The Recruitment-Worthy Intervention

She beats an invisible, contaminating vector with **exposure-control logic under heavy physiological influence** — the hazmat instinct of a person who realizes *it's in the air, cover your face, get them away from the source.* The ingenuity is the inference (the smell is the cause, dose tracks proximity) plus the improvisation (a wet cloth that she understands is only partial, so she works fast) plus the crude practical nerve to spray both the fixed guests and the creature itself. None of it is glamorous: a woman in a motel parking lot with a wet towel over her face and a can of dog spray, hurting victims to save them and driving an animal back into the dark.

The Branch's read (surfaced only in the WDS-200/205 report documents, clinical voice): a civilian who identified an unseen biological vector, applied exposure control, and remained functional enough to drive off the source while dosed — and who is the sole coherent account afterward. That combination — *named the invisible cause, controlled her exposure, acted while it was working on her* — is the recruitment-worthy quality. It also tells the Branch she is not immune; she is just fast, practical, and hard to stop.

## 10. Risks, Content Warnings, Tonal Pitfalls

**Content warnings (this sequence):**
- Explicit, degraded, unsafe sexual behaviour under a chemical/airborne influence — including the extreme vending-machine image (WDS-110) and the creature's grotesque anatomy (WDS-125). The guests are **victims reduced below the line of self-protection**, rendered with humanity. What must land is urgent harm and the horror of people chemically reduced below safety.
- The PC's own progressive, involuntary arousal and dulling judgment under the musk. The non-negotiable is that the **horror lands** (her body is being overwritten); whether it *also* reads as charged is the player's interpretation and is fine. We aim at the dread, not away from the heat (§0).
- **She has to hurt victims to save them.** Spraying the fixed guests (WDS-155) is awful and must read as awful-but-necessary triage, not casual or cool. It is part of the recruitment-worthy desperation, not a power fantasy.
- **No minors.** The affected guests are an unambiguously adult mix. No ambiguity in the prose.

> **Explicitness + placeholder convention (cross-cutting; see MANITOULIN §10 / PALE §10).** The default is that the drafting agent **writes the extreme beats very explicitly** — the vending-machine image (WDS-110) and the creature's anatomy (WDS-125) are the explicit/disturbing heart of the sequence and should not be softened. The `%%% HUMAN-DRAFT %%%` block is a **fallback only**: drop it in *solely if the drafting agent itself judges a specific beat to need more explicitness than it can comfortably write*, keeping the beat structure, links, and variable hooks intact around it, e.g.:
>
> ```
> /* %%% HUMAN-DRAFT %%%
>    WDS-110 — the woman at the vending machine.
>    Intent: in heat — soaked, whimpering, gratification-seeking; degraded and unsafe;
>    horror = a person reduced below safety by her own hijacked arousal. ~90 words.
>    %%% END %%% */
> ```
>
> **State the specifics in this design doc, not euphemisms.** If the design note can only gesture at a beat ("does something degraded") instead of naming what is depicted, that vagueness propagates straight into the prose — and it pinpoints exactly the beat a human may need to draft. The explicit content for WDS-110 and WDS-125 is therefore spelled out concretely in §2 (the soda can; the oversized dripping anatomy) rather than gestured at.

**Realism note — the spray.** Personal "pepper spray" marketed against people is illegal in 2003 Canada. **Bear spray is legal but is powerful enough to seriously or permanently injure — even kill — a human**, which is the wrong tool and the wrong implication here. Use **dog spray** ("dog-stop" / dog-deterrent spray): legal, ordinary, non-lethal, and plausibly on hand because of **posted coyote sightings in the area** (plant the notice at the motel desk in the WDS-090 table-setting beat). It backs an animal off without the lethality problem. The RCMP route most plausibly already carries it; the others picked up a small can after seeing the coyote warnings.

**Tonal pitfalls (from INCIDENTPLAN + this design):**
- **The creature's anatomy serves dread and contamination.** Render it explicit and grotesque; the target is animal wrongness and the visible source of the musk. Don't write *toward* or *against* whether a player finds it charged — just make the dread and contamination land (§0, "sell the dread").
- **Don't use clean werewolf lore.** The Branch documents morphology, odour, behaviour, and witness effects; it never names a folklore creature. Whether "werewolf rules" apply stays unknown to the PC and the player.
- **Don't make the guests comic.** Their hypersexual, degraded behaviour is frightening and unsafe; dignity returns when the influence breaks.
- **Don't frame her fear as prudishness** (sex-positivity rule, §0). Her dread is the chemical override of will and fear, never the sex itself.
- **Handle the vending-machine image as urgent harm**, not shock for its own sake — it sets the stakes (this reduces people below safety), and it should land that way.
- **Don't make her immune.** She feels the musk in her body and wins by inference plus exposure control, against a rising dose.
- **Keep the influence subjective.** The narrator never endorses the arousal/disinhibition as real desire — she *experiences* the pull, which is the horror (STYLE-GUIDE §13.5).
